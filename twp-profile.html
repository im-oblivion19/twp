Disposable Face Mask

Description

This is an image of a face mask. It is black in color and feels slightly crispy rather than smooth because it is disposable. I acquired this mask during the COVID quarantine, when my family bought a large pack of 150 face masks. We later switched to cotton reusable masks, so these disposable ones were rarely used. When I moved into my dorm for the Spring semester, I brought about 100 unused masks from that pack with me.

I first used one last week while cleaning a dusty area in my room. It works well for short-term use, but it is not very comfortable. Breathing through it feels like breathing through a thin napkin, and it has a slight smell. I am discarding it because it was used for a long time and also fell on the ground, making it dirty.

Anecdote

After sitting unused for nearly six years, it was finally time to wear the mask. I had been away from my dorm for a month, and the room needed cleaning. There was a gap between the drawer cabinet and wardrobe that had collected a significant amount of dust. I took a mask from the pack and put it on. As I adjusted it over my nose and under my chin, it immediately felt stuffy and fogged up my glasses.

That discomfort made sense, since masks are designed to filter particles from the air. While cleaning, it effectively prevented dust from entering my nose and mouth. After finishing, I discarded it because the front was likely covered in dust. As I removed it, I accidentally dropped it on the floor. In a college dorm environment, anything that falls on the floor should not be reused, so I threw it away.

Research

Facts:

  • About 75% of used face masks end up in landfills or floating in the sea.
  • COVID-19 led to a significant increase in the production of face masks.
